# Fundamentals - Second and Third Order Consequences

**Section:** Fundamental Analysis

## Key Takeaways

- First-order consequence thinking is face value: "the team is good, the community is strong, the market cap is small." This information is already priced in. Second and third-order thinking asks what each attribute causes into the future, which is where money is made.
- The Toshi thesis demonstrates multi-order thinking: Bitcoin ETF approved (first order) → Coinbase gets massive adoption (second order) → Coinbase users go to Base (third order) → they buy Toshi as the face of Base meme coin (fourth order) — each step was predictable through chain-of-consequence analysis.
- "If you find yourself always late to trades, it's because someone else in the market had considered what you were seeing now many steps ago" — being early is simply thinking further ahead than others.
- A great team is only valuable if you ask what specifically about the team is going to improve the coin's future — "an amazing team can pivot fast" is a second-order consequence, not the team quality itself.
- Adoption events are often bearish because the market prices in the expectation, then reality disappoints — Cardano's mainnet launch marked its peak, because the imagination of what it would be exceeded what it actually became.
- The thesis framework: buy by guessing and creating a scenario around what you think might happen, sell when the thesis either plays out (price reflects your predicted reality) or is invalidated (something changed your core assumption).
