## 11-taxes-and-banking-infrastructure — Trading Playbook Summary

### Core Concepts

- There are three types of taxation systems: citizenship-based (e.g., the US, which taxes citizens no matter where they live), residency-based (most of the Western world — UK, Europe, Canada, Australia — where tax is based on where you are resident), and territorial-based (where local income is taxed but foreign source income is exempt).
- The 183-day rule is a commonly cited threshold for establishing or avoiding tax residency, but the instructor emphasizes it is "a good rule of thumb" and "a guide not a rule" — countries also look at your permanent home, family ties, bank accounts, and other indicators of domicile.
- Personal and company tax residency are distinct: a company set up offshore but managed and controlled by a person still living in their home country will typically be treated as tax-resident in that home country under "management rules" — the offshore structure does not eliminate the tax liability.
- Controlled Foreign Corporation (CFC) rules exist in countries like Australia to prevent residents from sheltering profits indefinitely in offshore companies: undistributed profits you are entitled to can be taxed as if they were paid out to you.
- Crypto is treated differently depending on the country and how the activity is classified: in most jurisdictions, investing attracts capital gains tax (typically 20–30% in Western countries) while trading attracts higher personal income tax rates (typically 25–50%). Frequent swapping between coins is generally classified as trading, not investing.
- Every crypto trade or swap is a taxable event — not just when fiat is withdrawn. Countries that offer 0% tax on crypto (e.g., Portugal, Germany) often restrict that benefit to long-term investors; regularly swapping positions disqualifies the activity as investing.
- Building a functioning banking infrastructure — personal bank accounts and crypto exchange accounts registered to your new country of residence — is a critical and often overlooked part of any offshore setup. Having multiple exchange accounts and off-ramp options is essential.

### Rules

- "None of this should be considered tax advice — I'm just trying to make tax knowledge more accessible."
- "Just because you keep the money in a foreign bank account does not avoid this" — the company's tax residency follows where it is managed from, not where the money is held.
- "Every time you buy and sell a position it's considered a taxable event" — this applies to swaps between crypto assets, not only withdrawals to fiat.
- "If you buy Bitcoin and then 6 months later you swap into USDC or ETH, you're considered selling your position."
- "For most of you, you're going to need to buy and hold for at least a year" to benefit from 0% crypto tax treatment in applicable countries — but the instructor adds to "double check these countries' rules."
- On exit/departure taxes: "if your country has a departure or an exit tax you need to be looking at leaving now" — waiting until the peak of the bull run to leave means paying tax on the unrealized value of your crypto at the point of departure.
- After leaving a high-tax country, if you bought crypto before leaving, you "might need to stay away for usually about five years" before selling, to avoid that gain being taxed by your former country of residence.
- "The most secure setups involve someone living in one place with low taxes for the majority of the year as there's little space for judgment or interpretation."
- On tax strategy risk: "some tax strategies are really clear cut, others combine definitions from multiple countries and have elements of judgement in them, some altogether just illegal and rely on you not being found out."
- On the crypto paper trail: "the blockchain is literally a paper trail that can't be edited and can always be tracked" — governments have relationships with chainalysis firms, banks, and exchanges.
- On minimum spend thresholds: "if you've got $5,000 to your name, taxes is the last thing you should be worried about — you should be trying to get out of poverty."
- Pass-through entities (US LLCs, UK LLPs) are only tax-free if there are "no local operations" — defined as no employees, freelancers, offices, warehouses, or personal work performed from that country.

### Position Sizing / Risk Management

- The instructor uses a graphic to illustrate that taxes compound negatively against gains, and notes the key takeaway is "to illustrate the power tax can have of reducing your gains when you compound" and "the benefit of holding fewer positions for as long as possible."
- It is generally "much better to buy crypto personally for tax purposes" than through a business, as many 0% crypto tax countries apply that treatment to personal investing only.
- The instructor recommends setting up "multiple exchange accounts with multiple offboarding options" as a backup, citing the FTX collapse as a reminder: "it's always a good reminder to keep your crypto off exchanges."
- On when it is worth setting up offshore: if you stand to make multiple six figures in crypto, "you have the means to change your situation and pay 0% on your crypto gains." Approximate tax savings cited: 100k gain = 20–30k in taxes; 500k gain = 100–150k; 2.5M gain = 500–750k; 10M gain = 2–3M.
- The instructor's own offshore setup cost him approximately $40,000 to arrange, and he acknowledges simpler setups (e.g., a straightforward Dubai company) cost considerably less.

### Key Warnings / What Not To Do

- Do not assume an offshore company structure eliminates taxes if you continue to live in your home country: "they don't get to pay 0% taxes — the Dubai company should pay tax in the UK and they should keep paying their personal taxes in the UK too."
- Do not assume that having nominee directors removes management and control: "Steve thinks he's beating the system by having other people who are directors... [but] in order to change your personal tax residency to take advantage of these setups, you need to move country."
- Do not rely on a single exchange account or bank: the instructor explicitly warns against having to do "unnecessary verification at the peak of the bull run on a new exchange account because your other one asked you for a KYC questionnaire."
- Do not confuse holding crypto through a business as tax-efficient for crypto gains — "a lot of [0% crypto tax countries] are on the basis that it's personal investing," so holding via a company may negate the exemption.
- Do not treat the 183-day rule as a hard cutoff: "it is possible to spend 5 months in a country per year but have tax residency there" based on other ties like family, property, and bank accounts.
- Do not set up in a territorial tax country (e.g., Costa Rica, Thailand) while still actively managing an offshore company from within that country — "that income might not actually be considered foreign source because it's coming from an offshore company because you're managing it from that country." The instructor explicitly states this constitutes "a bit of tax evasion."
- Do not move offshore purely for tax reasons: "I really encourage you not to move solely for taxes — there is far more to life than just taxes. Consider moving somewhere and consider the lifestyle that you really want to live, and then try and integrate a tax strategy with it."
- Do not rush into an offshore setup without a custom plan: "the worst thing to do is to accidentally commit tax evasion."
- Be cautious with Cyprus as a banking jurisdiction: "back in 2008 [2013] the Cyprus government seized nearly half of people's deposits over 100k euros when the country was in economic crisis — it's not a really stable place to store your wealth."
