## 09-building-a-strategy-edge — Trading Playbook Summary

### Core Concepts

- The single best strategy in crypto is long-term investing: buying coins with strong fundamental conviction (5–100 million dollar market cap) and holding for 6–24 months, buying low when the market is fearful and selling high when the market is greedy. This strategy is described as "the centrepiece of every single crypto participant's portfolio" and is how the instructor and most crypto millionaires made their money.
- Every person in crypto needs a clearly defined strategy or edge — "buying the dip" is not a strategy. A strategy is "the principles and the system that you create that you follow and implement which gives you asymmetric upside." Without an edge you have no competitive advantage and will lose money.
- There are two paths to making money in crypto: follow the pro (join a high-level community, implement their system exactly, don't be selective) or become the pro (3–5 years of consistent study, mentors, refining your own edge). Staying an amateur is not an option.
- 99.9% of traders fail. Trading is described as "the hardest way to make money in existence." Profitable trading requires dedicating your entire life to it, experiencing a full market cycle firsthand, and caring only about the process — not the outcome. There is no part-time profitable trader.
- You cannot copy-trade your way to profits. Investing can be copied because it is time-insensitive; trading cannot be copied because time-sensitivity in entries and exits means a follower will consistently underperform or lose money.
- The instructor's edge is built on four strategies: (1) a long-term investing algorithm (Hunter algorithms) based on funding data and distance from moving average; (2) access to presale deal flow through a private syndicate; (3) automated airdrop farming with low-cost, high-wallet-count automation; (4) 100x altcoin investing using a fundamental analysis framework to find 5–100 million dollar market cap projects.
- A good strategy has five components: real data (not fake/arbitrary back-tested parameters), confluence across multiple datasets, intuition developed over time, network (to build and improve the system), and continuous refinement. 99% of algorithms are scams because they are built on fake data and back-tested parameter combinations, not data that actually impacts markets.

### Rules

- "You either follow the pro or become the pro. Anything outside of this is just gonna lead to losing money."
- "If you're gonna follow the pro, you have to do everything they say. Everything or nothing." — Do not be selective about which calls you follow.
- "There is no part-time profitable trader. You either dedicate your life to it or you avoid it completely."
- "Buying the dip is not a strategy" — a strategy requires predefined criteria for entry and exit, not a vague intention.
- "The coin isn't actually the really important component of the strategy, it's everything else around it" — strategy is the process of when to buy and sell, not which coin to buy.
- Do not trust any algorithm based purely on a back test: "You should never trust a system purely based off its back test. You need to find out, did it actually work from people implementing a live signal."
- "Funding data is the only data set which doesn't suffer from alpha decay" — it accurately reflects market sentiment regardless of how many people look at it.
- On presale timing: do deals during bear markets so that tokens unlock during the bull run. "If you do pre-sales here, you end up getting unlocked down here, you're probably going to have a way lower chance of making money."
- Strategy must have a range of inputs that still works if numbers are adjusted slightly — if changing one parameter number breaks the system entirely, it is fake data and a bad system.
- "You cannot experience a lesson from learning it from someone else and expect to avoid that mistake. Every mistake must be made from first hand experience in trading."

### Entry Signals

- For long-term investing (Hunter algorithms): a buy signal requires (1) price to go below a specific moving average and (2) negative funding data. Both conditions must be met simultaneously. Additional confluence datasets are checked after the signal to determine confidence level and position size.
- Signals are sent at the daily candle open, based on information locked in the prior closed daily candle. It is advised to wait 10–15 seconds after the daily open before acting on a signal.
- For altcoin selection: entry is determined by intuition after confirming conviction across fundamental criteria — fair launch, token distribution, narrative/niche, chain selection, team quality, community strength, and whether the project is new. The instructor explicitly waited for Toshi to reach a $2.5 million market cap entry, having initially seen it discussed at $30 million, based on thesis-building and intuition signaling a good entry.
- For identifying a buy entry range more generally: "You need to define a range of what you want to see in the data — maybe everyone really bearish, maybe funding data really low, maybe a couple other data sets really low — in order for you to buy. Define a range where you think the downside is limited and the upside is really high."
- Confluence across multiple data sources increases confidence: "The best traders I know all look for confluence." More data saying the same thing makes a signal more likely to be correct.

### Position Sizing / Risk Management

- Position sizing is influenced by the level of confluence observed at the time of a signal. The more data points confirming the signal, the larger the position. The instructor referenced entering Toshi with "half a percent of the supply" because of high confluence.
- For investments, stop losses are not used. Instead, an invalidation/validation framework is applied: "If a coin goes down 90%, it was invalidated before it went down 90%. If you were paying attention, it told you that it was not going up and you could have sold." The invalidation criteria minimizes downside by providing an exit point before a full decline.
- The validation (exit) criteria is thesis-based: you sell when the thesis plays out. For example, the instructor's Toshi exit thesis is selling "into the millions of users coming from Coinbase onto Base."
- For capital allocation by portfolio size: if under $2,000, focus entirely on airdrops and sub-$100 million market cap coins with 20x potential; if $2,000–$15,000, same focus, with free money opportunities added; if $15,000–$50,000, half in high-upside altcoins, with presales and free money opportunities; if $15,000–$200,000, airdrop farming should be automated or dropped, focus on presales and long-term investments; if over $200,000, a barbell strategy (mostly safe/major assets, 10–30% high upside smaller projects), no manual airdrop farming.
- The ultimate goal of all risk management and strategy-building is described as: "All good risk management strategy does is just cut out the big loss."

### Key Warnings / What Not To Do

- Do not day trade or swing trade unless you are willing to dedicate your entire life to it. "If you don't like the idea of this life [thinking about trading 24/7], then don't try to f****** trade."
- Do not copy traders. "You cannot make money long-term copying traders." The time-sensitivity of entries and exits means a follower will consistently exit later with less profit or sell after the price has already dropped.
- Do not blindly follow Twitter accounts or YouTubers as a strategy. "Influencers create liquidity, not alpha." Twitter accounts tell you when they buy but "never tell you when they sell." YouTubers focus on which coin to buy, missing the fact that the coin is not the important component.
- Do not follow too many people at once. "If you end up saying, okay, I'm gonna approach this bull run following the calls of four influencers, it's not gonna work" — conflicting signals from different strategies will produce losses.
- Do not be selective when following a pro. If you choose to follow someone's system, you must follow every call. "Anything you do by default is not gonna make money. And by default, if you do everything they do, it's gonna make money."
- Do not rely on a strategy built on arbitrary back-tested parameters. "Anyone can back test a random set of variables and make a seemingly accurate system." Moving average crossovers are cited as "the least reliable" algorithmic system. "If it was that easy, everyone would already be making money from it."
- Do not trust an algorithm without forward-tested results: "Backtesting means little to nothing because the markets are always changing and a system needs to work today."
- Do not focus on finding coins through surface-level dumpster-diving (e.g., looking at top-performing coin lists). "The process of finding good coins is like mostly just going through the dumpster... 95% of research is very much just surface level searching through the dumpster." Instead, use a trusted network to pre-filter and apply a fundamental analysis framework.
- Do not make emotional decisions instead of process-based decisions. "In the bad strategy, you buy because you're feeling greed... there's no justification for why it gives you asymmetric returns."
- Do not over-focus on which coin to buy while ignoring the process. "People always focus in a bad strategy on the coin, but they don't focus on the process... you can triple your money in a bull run with the worst coins in crypto by having a really great process/strategy/edge."
- Do not pursue yield farming, staking, or mining as primary strategies: "We're here for delta." The upside of staking (6–20%) does not justify the risk of total loss.
