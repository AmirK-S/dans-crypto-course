## 08-technical-analysis — Trading Playbook Summary

### Core Concepts

- Technical analysis makes up approximately 20% of the instructor's overall investment process; the remaining 80% is fundamental research. The instructor explicitly states: "technical analysis is like 20% of what I do."
- Chart analysis is used primarily for three purposes: (1) spotting entry points, (2) identifying exits, and (3) understanding which phase of the market cycle a coin is in.
- The strength of a coin's fundamentals and community is reflected in its price action — newer, stronger coins produce cleaner technical setups and higher win rates than old, weak coins like Litecoin.
- Support and resistance is the foundation of chart analysis. The instructor uses only two types of lines: breakouts from a downtrend and horizontal support/resistance. He draws support at candle closes, not wicks, on the 4-hour and daily timeframes.
- Volume is a proxy for attention and interest. Higher volume on upward moves is bullish; declining volume on downward moves is bullish; low volume breakouts and high volume declines are bearish signals.
- Retracements and rallies follow rough, repeating patterns depending on the coin type (Bitcoin, Ethereum, Solana, or meme coins). The instructor measures historical average retracements to set expectations for when to buy dips.
- Capitulation events — driven by intense fear, forced selling, or cascading liquidations — represent some of the best buying opportunities. The instructor uses multiple data tools (hunter algorithm, realized losses, long liquidations, bid/ask depth, DEX volume, funding rates) to detect capitulation.

### Rules

- "The less you can use charts, I think, the better" as an investor — the instructor recommends charts only as a supporting tool, not the primary decision-making framework.
- "Not all lines are made equal. Not every line has the same value" — the instructor only draws lines that are actually important after six years of experience.
- Support and resistance lines are drawn where candle bodies close, not where wicks extend, especially on higher timeframes.
- The instructor only does chart analysis on the 4-hour and daily timeframes.
- "Each additional tap [of a support level] is a sign of weakness" — the first test of a support level is the strongest because it has the largest concentration of buyers; subsequent tests drain those buyers.
- "The harder it is to buy the dip, the stronger the trend and more likely the coin goes up."
- For selling, the instructor does not use resistance levels on small/meme coins: "I sell when the thesis plays out. I don't really give a f*** about what the resistance is." Stop losses are thesis-based, not chart-based: "I don't set a 5% stop loss on investments. It's about the thesis."
- Volume should increase as price increases, and decrease as price decreases for a healthy bullish trend. The four volume/price combinations: price up + volume up = good; price down + volume up = bad; price up + volume down = bad; price down + volume down = good.
- "You want to see volume come in on the breakout and then you want to be buying in on the retest."
- On breakout/retest strategy: "You want to see a big volume spike" on the breakout — a low-volume breakout is not worth buying.
- Do not use Binance volume data for larger coins — it may not be accurate. Volume data is most reliable and useful for smaller meme coins.
- "No one will sell a coin lower than a forced seller" — forced selling and capitulation represent true bottoms because those sellers accept whatever price the market gives.
- For the hunter algorithm buy signal: "When funding is negative and price is below the green line, we look to buy."
- Capitulation data tools should not be used in isolation: "You shouldn't use these tools by themselves. These tools I use them all together and they all give confidence to whoever I decide to buy or sell."
- Basic support/resistance analysis does not work well on Bitcoin and large-cap coins — more advanced off-chart data is needed for those.

### Entry Signals

- **Breakout and retest:** Price breaks out above a key resistance level on high volume, then retests that level as support. The instructor buys on the retest. This is his preferred setup for meme coins and small-cap coins.
- **Sizable dip / retracement buy:** Buy after a coin has pulled back by its historically typical percentage. Rough guidelines stated by the instructor:
  - Bitcoin: ~20% pullback in a bull market
  - Ethereum: ~25% pullback in a bull market
  - Solana: ~30–50% pullback in a bull market
  - Meme coins: ~40–60% pullback in a bull market (sometimes 70%)
- **Bear market accumulation:** Wait for altcoins to decline 80–90% before looking for entries after a bear market.
- **Capitulation buy (top 300 coins / Bitcoin):** Buy when multiple capitulation signals align — hunter algorithm buy signal (negative funding + price below moving average), realized losses going deeply negative, large long liquidations, a spike in bid depth showing smart money accumulating, high DEX volume spikes, and highly negative aggregate funding rates.
- The instructor explicitly states these are used in confluence: "I would obviously combine this analysis with my Hunter algorithm and other things that I look at to warrant me to buy something."

### Position Sizing / Risk Management

- The instructor does not use a fixed percentage stop loss on investments: "I don't set a 5% stop loss on investments. It's a bad idea. It's about the thesis. The thesis is what gives you the sell region."
- Stop loss / exit is determined by thesis invalidation, not chart levels.
- For traders (as distinguished from investors), the chart can be used to determine a risk/reward setup — but the instructor does not apply this to his own investing approach.
- Expected return ranges stated by the instructor to guide position management:
  - Bitcoin: 4–8x from cycle bottom to top
  - Ethereum: similar to Bitcoin, slightly more
  - Solana: 15–30x from cycle bottom to top
  - Meme coins: 10–100x from cycle bottom to top
- These ranges are used to calibrate when to begin reducing risk: "When you get up to those levels, you're gonna think obviously it can keep going... but if you want to look at hey, hold on a second, it's done its usual pump, maybe this is somewhere where you'd want to be taking off risk."

### Key Warnings / What Not To Do

- Do not rely solely on technical analysis as an investor: "If you're an investor in crypto and all you do is just use charts, that's a bad sign."
- Do not apply basic support/resistance analysis (breakouts, retests) to Bitcoin or large-cap coins — "good luck doing that on here" — it does not work reliably; more advanced data is required for large coins.
- Do not trade or invest in old, weak coins like Litecoin using technical setups: "The reason this was such a bad coin to try to trade was because it was a weak coin." Weak coins produce unreliable technical patterns.
- Do not draw too many lines or attribute equal value to every line you draw: "A lot of people will do super f****** stupid lines like all the time."
- Do not use Binance volume data for analysis — it is not accurate. Be cautious of wash trading and derivatives volume on larger coins.
- Do not buy a 20% dip on a meme coin as if it is a normal retracement: "Why are you buying a 20% dip on a meme coin? You know the average retracement."
- Do not sell during capitulation events out of fear: "Smart people buy fear. Smart people like us buy capitulation." The instructor recounts selling into the COVID crash as a mistake.
- Do not use capitulation tools in isolation — they must be used together to confirm a signal.
- If a coin spends a very long time at support without going up, that is a sign of weakness, not strength: "If a coin spends a lot of time at support, it's probably not worth buying."
