# Chasing pumps

**Section:** Do-NOT-Do-List
**Loom ID:** 9e9c7c66fa984d0bb1699e882cc4aa89

## Key Takeaways

- In a bull run everything eventually goes up, so chasing a coin that's already pumping usually means selling your laggard right before it has its own rally — emotional decisions at the worst possible time.
- The critical distinction: selling a loser to buy a winner is a logic-based, process-driven decision; chasing a pump is an emotion-based, reactive decision — the outcome of these two looks similar but the long-term result is completely different.
- Correct process for switching positions: identify underlying strength in the new asset, assess second-and-third-order catalysts (e.g., upcoming conference, narrative shift), confirm the new asset has asymmetric upside before acting.
- Questions to ask before selling: Has the underlying thesis changed? Is the upside still asymmetric in the new coin? Am I being driven by herd mentality? Is my current coin a short-term laggard or a long-term underperformer?
- The worst shitcoins pump last in a bull run — when coins like Ripple, Litecoin, Cardano, and Tron go ballistic, that is often the top signal, not a reason to rotate into them.
- When Solana was doubled-down at $20, it was based on: top-3 mindshare vs. top-7 price, upcoming Breakpoint conference catalyst, and clear price action outperformance vs. Bitcoin — all logical, forward-looking factors.
