## 06-do-not-do-list — Trading Playbook Summary

### Core Concepts

- Emotions work against you in markets — they cause buying at tops and selling at bottoms. The only value of an emotion is recognizing when you need to do the opposite of what you feel.
- There is a critical difference between emotional decisions and process-based decisions. Selling a laggard to buy a winner is only valid when it is logic-driven (thesis change, underlying strength, second/third order consequences), not when it is driven by pain avoidance.
- Copying Twitter or YouTube influencers is not a viable strategy. By the time an influencer has high conviction on a coin, most of the outperformance has already played out. Influencers also tell you when to buy but not when to sell — and the exit is the most important part.
- Gaining conviction as prices rise is a mistake for long-term investors. Asymmetric upside only exists when buying near the bottom during fear. As price rises, more people are in profit and the path of least resistance shifts downward.
- Buying or selling without an edge is equivalent to gambling without house odds — you may make money in a bull run, but you will give it back because the 20-year averaged return of edge-less decisions is zero or negative.
- Selling a position to try to buy it back lower almost never works. The brain generates this idea at or near the bottom, driven by pain avoidance. Winning coins will not give you a re-entry; you end up forced into laggards.
- Mixing strategies destroys returns. Long-term investing, momentum trading, and day trading each have different and incompatible rules for entries, exits, stop losses, and time horizons. Applying the rules of one strategy while executing another is a guaranteed path to losses.
- Selling half on a 2x is not risk management — it is an arbitrary, statistically unbacked rule that cuts the upside of home run investments while providing negligible downside protection.

### Rules

- "The only way to buy low in the green zone and sell in the red zone is to ignore emotions and listen to logic."
- "You have to buy when it hurts and you have to sell when it hurts" (as a long-term investor).
- "Buying fear is scary upfront and peaceful later. Buying greed is easy upfront and scary later."
- "Every coin in a bull run goes up" — do not sell an underperforming coin purely out of emotion; it will likely have its turn.
- "It's not about the coin, it's about the portfolio, the process, the thesis and the consideration of downside upfront."
- "Winners tend to keep winning" — do not cut a winning position early with an arbitrary rule.
- "Let your winners run and cut your losers."
- "The three times you sell: you were right, you were wrong, you found something better." These are the only valid reasons to exit an investment.
- "The big money is not made in the individual fluctuations but in the main movement" (quoted from Reminiscences of a Stock Operator).
- "If you're investing in an asset with a long-term target, stick to it. It happens or it doesn't. Be careful adding more variables thinking you can time the market." (Angelo BTC tweet, cited approvingly)
- Do not use a stop loss on long-term investments — the stop loss is your thesis invalidation. If your thesis no longer holds, that is when you sell.
- When doing momentum trading, you must use a stop loss because you are buying high and asymmetric downside exists.
- Be clear on what your strategy is and do not mix strategies. If you are a long-term investor, buy low and sell high exclusively. If you are a momentum trader, buy breakouts exclusively.
- "You make the most money by doing nothing." The best stock portfolios historically belong to people who never sold.
- "It's not about the individual punches, it's about the whole game" — focus on winning the bull run, not on individual short-term moves.

### Entry Signals

- For long-term investing: buy only during peak fear, near the bottom, when upside is asymmetric — meaning limited downside and large potential upside. "You can only buy in this green box" (the early, fearful phase of the cycle).
- A valid trigger to shift capital between coins is when you can answer "yes" to: Does my preferred coin have more conviction and a stronger thesis than what I currently hold? Is the price action showing underlying strength (e.g., outperforming on low timeframe)? Is there a fundamental catalyst (e.g., upcoming conference, narrative shift)?
- Questions to ask before any buy or sell: Did it hurt to buy or sell here? Am I doing the easy thing or the hard thing? Is this decision based on logic or emotion? Could I imagine a professional making this decision? Who has the better thesis — the buyer or the seller?
- For momentum trading: buy breakouts (buying high to sell higher), with a defined stop loss and take profit.
- For day trading: enter based on technical setups at any point in the market cycle, with a stop loss and take profit preset.

### Position Sizing / Risk Management

- Do not add more capital to the market as the bull run progresses. "As the bull run gets later and later, you should be reducing your risk, not increasing it." Adding money at higher prices raises your cost basis and can turn an early profitable position into a loss.
- "You always want to have a little bit more than what feels comfortable" in terms of position size — enough to have a meaningful lifestyle impact, but not so much that emotions destroy your decision-making.
- Do not sell half on a 2x. This is described as "one of the dumbest strategies" because it removes upside from home run investments. The goal in crypto is asymmetric returns — risk a little to make a lot. Cutting winners early violates this.
- The correct way to manage risk in long-term investing is: (1) wait for asymmetric upside before entering; (2) monitor whether the thesis remains valid; (3) sell when the thesis plays out, is invalidated, or a clearly better opportunity exists.
- "Your ability to make money comes down to your ability to cut your losers early and let your winners run." Make sure the times you are wrong have a very small portfolio impact and the times you are right have a very large portfolio impact.
- Selling to buy back lower increases the number of decisions you must make, and "each additional decision that you make is added risk in the portfolio." More decisions means more chances to get something wrong.
- Selling and re-buying also increases taxes. In Australia, holding under one year triggers short-term capital gains tax, which is significantly higher than long-term capital gains tax for positions held over one year.
- Aim for concentrated, high-conviction bets. "There's only 2 to 3 really really good high conviction bets" available at any time. Spreading into too many coins after taking partial profits leads to standardized market returns and an inability to time individual exits.

### Key Warnings / What Not To Do

- Do not chase pumps based on emotion. "When you chase a pump, you are making an emotional decision." The worst coins pump last in a bull run, and chasing them means selling your position just as it is about to have its turn.
- Do not make emotional decisions. Emotions will have you "buying when you're supposed to be selling and selling when you're supposed to be buying."
- Do not copy your favourite Twitter influencer or YouTube guru for coin picks. "Copying your favourite crypto guru on social media" is not a viable strategy. Influencers tell you when to buy but never when to sell. Most online mentions of coins are paid or coordinated shills designed to create FOMO so the influencer can exit. "Your influencer's exit liquidity. Literally."
- Do not gain conviction with higher prices (for long-term investors). "If something goes 3x, you shouldn't even be trying to buy it." Interest in buying should decrease as price increases.
- Do not buy or sell without an edge. "The big money in booms is always made first by the public on paper. And it remains on paper." Making money without an edge is temporary — you are "borrowing money from the smart and giving it back to them when they need it."
- Do not sell a coin to try to buy it back lower. "Some basic beginner investor, amateur, thinks that they can sell a coin and then try and buy it back lower before it goes back up again. No, it's just not gonna happen." If you own a winning coin, it will not give you a re-entry.
- Do not mix strategies. Do not enter a position using momentum/trader logic (buying a breakout) and then hold it using long-term investor logic (no stop loss, no exit plan). "Letting your trades that go wrong become investments is a surefire sign of a lack of strategy."
- Do not sell half on a 2x. "The key to being a profitable investor is to let your winners run, and cut your losers. So if you sell half on a double, you've just sold the winner early." It creates gambling mentality ("playing with house money"), increases position count, leads to underperformance, and is not real risk management.
- Do not look at charts all day. "Looking at charts all day is just gonna make you less money. You make the most money by doing nothing."
- Do not copy-trade signals from external sources without understanding entries and exits. "It's not about the calls, it's not about the coins... it's about what you do alongside these things."
- Do not increase capital deployed into the market as the bull run matures. The reason you are making money is because you entered when upside was asymmetric. Adding capital at higher prices destroys that asymmetry and raises overall cost basis.
