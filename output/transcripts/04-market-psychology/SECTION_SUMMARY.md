## 04-market-psychology — Trading Playbook Summary

### Core Concepts
- Market cycles repeat because all humans share the same cognitive biases and instinctual programming — the same flaws drive the same behaviors every cycle, roughly every four years in crypto.
- The primary cognitive biases that harm investors are: confirmation bias (seeking information that confirms existing beliefs), bandwagon effect (copying the herd), anchoring (clinging to the first piece of information received), hindsight bias (overestimating past predictive ability), deprival super-reaction (refusing to sell at a loss to avoid accepting money was taken), inconsistency-avoidance tendency (resisting belief changes), and doubt-avoidance tendency (ignoring negative information on holdings).
- Markets are forward-looking, not a fair reflection of current value — price moves in anticipation of future events, not in response to them. "You make money in markets by doing today what people are going to recognize tomorrow."
- "Buy the rumor, sell the news" — smart investors buy before an announcement and sell before or when the news comes out; by the time news is public, it is already priced in.
- Regret is an inevitable outcome in markets — perfection is impossible, and the goal is to be "consistently good enough," maximizing for making roughly 80% of possible gains 100% of the time rather than chasing perfection and missing entirely.
- When you buy in the market, you will always feel pain — the choice is between the pain of buying at the bottom (pain upfront, profit later) or the pain of buying near the top out of confidence (easy upfront, losses later). "Buying fear is scary upfront and peaceful later. Buying greed is easy upfront and scary later."
- The market moves in the direction that hurts the most people — it is designed to shake participants out of good positions before price moves in their favor. "Dead people are some of the best investors in the world" because the market cannot convince them to participate at the wrong time.

### Rules
- "The goal is you identify cognitive biases and you recognize them when you feel them for yourself and you ignore them and you act against them. That's how you make money in markets."
- "In markets you get paid for doing the opposite of what your instincts tell you to do. You get rich ignoring the evolutionary evolved instincts which no longer protect you but harm you."
- Do the opposite of the herd — the best time to buy is when no one wants to buy; the best time to sell is when everyone is doing well and sentiment is at its most positive.
- Contrarian positioning only works at extremes — "in the middle of the trend, the trend is your friend." Do not counter-trade the middle of a bull market; being contrarian applies at tops and bottoms, not throughout.
- Every seller is usually a future buyer — at the bottom, all sellers have moved to cash and will eventually want to buy back in, which is what causes the next move up.
- "You cannot come to the market and expect the price to be a reflection of anything fair. The price is simply a representation of what humans are thinking, feeling, and seeing, and anticipating to happen in the future."
- When you feel regret about a missed trade, do not ask "how do I get into this coin?" — ask "where did I go wrong in my system so I can avoid making this mistake the next 100 times it comes up?" Fix the root cause, not the individual instance.
- "If you have a system, you never say I regret not buying because the system didn't find the coin." Follow a system consistently; regret signals the absence of a system.
- Do not attach your identity to any particular coin (e.g., adding a coin name to your Twitter handle) — it makes critical thinking harder when it is time to exit.
- Always focus on the downside first: "The best investors always look at the downside. They always ask themselves, what could go wrong? How would I lose? How could this go badly?"
- "The most profitable strategy in crypto is to face pain up front, to consider all the risks and upside before the position is even entered. You should mentally be considering 50 different what-if scenarios before your entry occurs" (for long-term investments).
- "The market can remain irrational longer than you can remain solvent" — do not counter-trade an ongoing bull trend expecting the market to immediately correct.

### Entry Signals
- Buy when sentiment is at its worst and the most people hate a coin — "the best time to buy is when no one wants to buy. More people can hate the coin. How many more people can sell? You get to a point where there's no one left to hate, there's no one left to sell, there's no more bad news to come. And the only possible circumstance after the worst has happened is for things to get better."
- At the market bottom, the ratio of cash-holders wanting to buy vastly outnumbers coin-holders wanting to sell — when everyone has sold and there are very few remaining sellers, that is the signal to be holding a position.
- Buy before a major announcement, not after — smart investors buy in anticipation of news and sell "three to seven days before the announcement comes out," because the announcement itself is the top.
- The pain felt during the buying decision is a proxy for entry quality — "the more pain you feel upfront with making a decision in the market, the better you're setting yourself up for the long term." High upfront pain indicates a good entry; easy, confident buying near the top indicates a poor entry.
- Look for situations where the market has not yet priced in a narrative you believe is high-probability — "I saw a future, I forward looked into the future, and I thought this was a high probability scenario that none of the market was pricing in."

### Position Sizing / Risk Management
- The overall strategy taught in this course is described as: "find and spot 10-100x opportunities, buy them low using a hands-off strategy, hold them through the course of a bull run, and then exit near the top."
- Minimize the amount of active participation — "you should only be buying low a few times and then waiting and then selling out. The key to long-term success in the markets is to minimize the amount of participation that you have to do."
- Do not sell a losing position simply to avoid psychological pain — but also do not let the refusal to realize a loss (deprival super-reaction bias) prevent critical re-evaluation of a position's fundamentals.
- Aim to maximize for making approximately 80% of maximum possible gains consistently, rather than attempting to perfectly time tops and bottoms and risking missing entirely.
- Explicit numerical position sizing or percentage-based risk rules are not explicitly covered in this section's transcripts.

### Key Warnings / What Not To Do
- Do not use confirmation bias to justify holding a losing position — "you look at all the new information coming out about the coin... you use these updates as confirmation bias that the coin's gonna make you money. But what you fail to recognize is if you didn't own the coin you probably wouldn't have bought if you had heard this information."
- Do not copy what everyone on Twitter or social media is saying about a coin — "if you just copy what all your Twitter is saying, you're doing the opposite of what you should be doing."
- Do not add a coin's name or branding to your identity (e.g., social media bio) — "it makes that a part of your identity. It's harder to think critically... you need to be fluid and you need to not attach your identity to any particular coin."
- Do not use hindsight bias as a source of false competence — "if you didn't buy, there's a floor in your system and you need to fix it. Don't let this bias of 'I almost bought it so I'm pretty much good enough' make you complacent."
- Do not ignore negative news on a project you are holding — "negative news on a project leads you to avoid delving deeper into the negative news, preferring not to entertain the possibility that the project is in trouble."
- Do not buy when it feels good and confident, expecting that to be a good long-term entry — "trying to enter when you had confidence is what f***** you up."
- Do not try to counter-trade the middle of a bull market — "the trend is your friend is a specific term that can only be applied during the middle of the bull market."
- Do not ask "how do I get into a coin I missed?" when you experience regret — "when you make a mistake and you regret something, you don't ask how do I fix this one instance where I f***** up? You ask, where did I go wrong in my system?"
- Do not apply linear price projections to recent trends — "humans have a tendency to project out linear valuations based off of recent trends" (recency bias), and this causes investors to chase momentum at the wrong time and miss turning points.
- Do not over-participate in the market — "the market forces participation... it wants to shake you out, take your money, and then go up." Every additional transaction or reaction to market noise increases the chance of being shaken out.
