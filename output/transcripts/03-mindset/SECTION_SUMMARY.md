## 03-mindset — Trading Playbook Summary

### Core Concepts

- **Markets are zero-sum (or negative-sum):** "Every dollar that you make is a dollar that someone else lost." Crypto is negative-sum because platforms (exchanges) extract fees on every transaction. The only period markets become effectively positive-sum is during "exit liquidity" (the PVE phase), when new, inexperienced participants flood in and existing holders can all profit simultaneously at their expense.
- **Two market modes — PVP and PVE:** Player-vs-player (bear markets) is a closed system where existing participants compete against each other; the goal is to survive and accumulate. Player-vs-enemy (bull market peak with new retail inflow) is when experienced participants can all profit together off "exit liquidity" — the new, naive investors entering for the first time.
- **Emotions destroy profits:** Human beings are hard-wired for herd behavior, which is the opposite of what profitable investing requires. You must "buy when it hurts and sell when it hurts" — i.e., do the opposite of what your instincts and the crowd are doing. Profits come from exploiting the emotional and cognitive inefficiencies of other market participants.
- **Pattern recognition database and intuition:** Words and courses can only convey roughly 2% of what you need to know. The rest must be downloaded through lived experience and mistakes. The highest form of decision-making in investing is intuition — "a perpetually accumulated wisdom from lived experiences which manifest in our subconscious mind." Beginners rely on instinct (which runs opposite to good investing); experts rely on intuition built from thousands of hours of first-hand experience.
- **It's not about the coin, it's about the process:** Every coin, even the best-performing ones, has lost money for people who lacked a process. Without a defined strategy, edge, entry/exit criteria, emotional management, and position sizing, any money made will eventually be returned to the market. "A great investor can make money on any coin because of good process."
- **Preparedness, not predictions:** Price targets are a facade. "The best investors hypothesize both a validation and invalidation thesis behind the asset they buy and then they enter when the upside is asymmetric and then they exit when the thesis is played out." There are too many variables for price predictions to be reliable, and they remove the flexibility needed to adapt.
- **Everyone pays their tuition:** "There is a minimum amount of losses, a minimum amount of pain, a minimum amount of mistakes that you must make, over a minimum amount of time, before you can keep all of the money that you're making." Approximately 80% of the lessons needed to be a profitable investor can only be learned through first-hand experience — not from a course. The typical tuition period is one full market cycle of three to four years.

### Rules

- "You don't make money in crypto... You take money. You're taking other people's money." Internalize that every gain comes from another participant's loss.
- "During PVP [bear market], you want to survive. You're not trying to get rich here." Accumulate long-term positions during bear markets; do not blow up capital.
- "Your goal during exit liquidity is to be smart enough... to take on risk, and then it is to exit." Sell to the retail/normie wave — "exit into the liquidity of the normies."
- "You have to follow a process. You have to have a strategy. You have to have a defined edge... You follow the logic, not the emotions."
- "You need to retrain your brain to buy when it hurts and sell when it hurts." If it feels good to buy, you are likely paying a premium; if it feels terrible to buy, you are likely getting a discount.
- "Don't use leverage — stick to spot." Leverage amplifies emotional decisions and leads to ruin for the vast majority of participants.
- "Never invest more than you're willing to lose, especially in your first market cycle."
- "The goal is not to buy the bottom or sell the top... The goal is to capture six-eighths of the trend." Trying to buy the exact bottom or sell the exact top is perfectionism that "massively decreases probability of success."
- "Selling tops is way harder than buying bottoms." Rather than trying to sell the top, the instructor asks himself daily toward the later stages of a bull run: "Do I think this is the final eighth of the bull run?" When the answer is yes, he begins implementing his exit strategy.
- "You should always assume your portfolio is 30% less than it is" because you will never sell the exact top. For more volatile altcoins, assume the position is worth 50% less.
- "Invest in preparedness, not in prediction" (quoting Nassim Taleb). Be prepared for all scenarios — both validation and invalidation of your thesis — rather than relying on price targets.
- "You have to go internal to external" in your research — form your own thesis first, then seek external input. Doing the reverse (going to Twitter or YouTube to find coins first) means you are outsourcing conviction and will never know when to sell.
- Focus exclusively on delta (price appreciation), not yield. "100% capital risk to make 5-20% never makes sense." The best yield is the price going up — buy low, sell high, keep capital liquid.
- When you make a mistake, ask: "What went wrong in my system and how can I stop this from ever happening again?" Focus on long-term solutions (fixing the root cause), not just short-term fixes (patching the immediate error).

### Entry Signals

- Enter a position "when the upside is asymmetric" and "when the coin is not a consensus bet / majority aren't interested." Waiting for these two conditions to be met is the core entry discipline taught.
- Do not buy a coin simply because it appeared on Twitter or a YouTube influencer discussed it. "If you've found the coin that you want to buy from Twitter, you've already f***ed up. Because any coin that you're seeing shilled by big accounts on Twitter has already had a large portion of its upside priced in."
- Buy when it is painful and uncomfortable — "your best investments will be the hardest to buy in the moment." If buying feels easy or comfortable (rating below an 8 out of 10 on a pain scale), the upside is likely not sufficiently asymmetric.
- The instructor describes his own buy signals as triggering "when I at least want to buy" — i.e., when he is most reluctant. He uses an algorithmic system that sends phone alerts to hold him accountable against his own emotions.
- For bottoms: rather than trying to time the exact bottom, buy "when you think the downside is limited and the upside is asymmetric." Bottoms never feel like bottoms — "it's always going to feel like you're buying before a cliff."
- Use second and third order consequence thinking to get into positions before the majority of the market understands the thesis. "You want to find multiple step-ahead consequences which very few people are planning for, that you believe has a high probability of occurring."
- For outsized returns: find coins no one on Twitter is talking about, that are "hard to buy" (e.g., not yet listed on major exchanges), and where you are thinking multiple narrative steps ahead of the consensus.

### Position Sizing / Risk Management

- "Decrease your position size so that you're not gonna end up making emotional decisions due to the increased emotion of really caring about the money that you've put into the market." Size positions to a level where a 50% loss would not cause you to act emotionally.
- "Professionals optimize for total portfolio returns. They don't care about what multiple they can get on one coin." Avoid spreading capital thinly across many coins hoping for one 100x; instead, size positions based on risk/reward and your ability to spot asymmetric opportunities.
- "10% of your decisions will bring you 90% of your profits." Wait; do nothing most of the time. "In investing, the right thing to do is almost always nothing." Act only when the setup is genuinely asymmetric.
- Volatility is not risk in itself — "real risk is permanent loss of money." Be willing to hold through large drawdowns (the instructor held Solana through multiple 40–50%+ retracements on the way to his target) as long as the long-term thesis remains intact and you are not dealing with losing initial capital.
- "Your goal is to position early into an asset's uptrend so that temporary downside volatility eats into profits, not initial capital."
- "Either do yield plus delta or just delta." Do not come to crypto purely to collect yield due to the risks of losing principal capital (smart contract risk, platform risk, liquidity risk, locked capital preventing optimal exits).
- Minimize losses per mistake, especially in early market cycles. "Make as many mistakes as possible with the least amount of loss per mistake... reduce the capital risk per mistake so that you can stay in the game."

### Key Warnings / What Not To Do

- Do not HODL shitcoins: "The hodl meme that you see online is bullshit. Outside of maybe Bitcoin, you shouldn't be trying to implement something related to hodl because every shitcoin's end destination is minus 99%."
- Do not use leverage: "Don't use leverage, stick to spot." Leverage is compared to a gambling addiction — "gambling grandma."
- Do not copy random influencers on Twitter: "Random dudes on Twitter tell you when they buy but they never tell you when they sell." You will have an entry but no exit, which is worse than doing nothing.
- Do not ask for or rely on price predictions: "Stop asking for price predictions. They don't work." Price targets create arbitrary exit levels that have no relationship to actual market dynamics and cause people to either exit too early or bag-hold through the bear market.
- Do not try to sell the exact top: "Selling a top has a 98% plus failure rate." Trying to get a perfect exit leaves you exposed to the extreme volatility of bull market peaks, where altcoins can drop 50% in a week.
- Do not chase yield at the expense of capital liquidity: locking up money to earn yield prevents you from taking profit at optimal times and exposes you to total loss from smart contract bugs, platform failures, or liquidity pool exploits. The instructor illustrates this with a case study showing it can take 48 years to recover yield losses if the underlying asset drops 88%.
- Do not invest more than you are willing to lose, especially in your first market cycle: most first-cycle participants will lose the majority or all of their capital due to the Dunning-Kruger peak of overconfidence and the unavoidable "tuition" the market charges.
- Do not approach crypto as an amateur (no strategy, no back-testing, no defined edge, no mentor, emotional decision-making, treating it like a casino): "If you are an amateur in crypto, you will lose all of your money."
- Do not focus only on short-term solutions when you make mistakes. Always also ask what systemic change you need to make so the mistake does not recur: "You're pulling weeds out of the garden from the surface, not from the roots."
- Do not sell a position during a bull market just to mitigate short-term volatility: "Selling to mitigate the volatility to the downside on an asset you believe goes higher is trying to maximise for preserving 20–40% of your money and potentially missing 1000x upside."
